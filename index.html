Operating Rules
01
Every hour produces something real
No passive learning. Reading counts only if it feeds a decision or artifact you make that same session. Watch tutorials only while building the thing the tutorial teaches.
02
Gate yourself hard
Each phase gate deliverable must exist in the physical world — live automation, signed client, real payment — before the next phase starts. No exceptions, no partial credit.
03
Sell before you build
Never build a system for a client who hasn't agreed to pay. Your first prospect conversation happens in Week 3 — before your stack is finished. Close first, build second.
04
One vertical first
HVAC and plumbing only until you have 3 paying clients. Depth in one vertical beats shallow presence in five. Auto repair enters at Phase 3 and not before.
05
Block the hours before the week starts
Schedule your 5–7 hours Sunday night. Treat them like a Parsons meeting. Miss two consecutive weeks → write a post-mortem on why before continuing. No journaling = no advancing.
06
Document everything that breaks
Every bug, every client objection, every failed outreach message is content. Log it in the Journal below. These become your playbook, your pitch armor, and your case study material.
When You Get Stuck
⚡ The Unstick Protocol
01Name the exact blocker in one sentence. Vague stuck ("I don't know what to do") is a different problem than specific stuck ("my Make scenario fails when the form field is empty"). Write it down.
02Technical issue: search the Make.com community forum first (5 min), then Claude (10 min), then the specific tool docs. If not resolved in 25 min total, post in the Make community with a screenshot of your scenario.
03Sales stuck (no responses, no interest): go back to conversation notes. What exact words did owners use about their problem? Rewrite your outreach using their language, not yours.
04Motivation stuck: re-read Rule 01. Open the Journal. Write one sentence about why you started this. Then do the smallest next action on the task list — not the whole week, just the next checkbox.
05If a phase gate feels unreachable after 3 extra weeks: reduce scope. A smaller gate you pass is infinitely better than a perfect gate you never reach. Document the gap and advance.

WK 02 Build Your First Automation (Intake Flow) ✅ 0/7 6–7 hrs ▼
Goal: A real, working Make.com scenario that captures a service request, runs it through Claude, and logs it. Ugly is fine. Working is required.
Build

  • Document every step you built in a Google Doc — this becomes your delivery template for client #1

WK 05 Outreach Blitz — 5 Real Conversations ✅ 0/6 5–6 hrs ▼
Goal: 5 real owner conversations this week. Show the Loom to anyone who shows any interest. Zero pitch pressure — pure discovery and proof of life.
Outreach

WK 06 Close Attempt + Phase Gate Review ✅ 0/6 5–6 hrs ▼
Goal: Push any warm prospects toward a decision. Run an honest Phase 1 retrospective. Gate check — you don't advance until both gate conditions are real.
Close
  • Follow up every "interested" prospect with a pilot offer: "I'll set it up free for 2 weeks. If you don't see results, you owe me nothing — and I just ask for honest feedback and permission to use the numbers in a case study."
  • If no takers yet: expand to 10 more prospects. Not a failure — pipeline is a numbers game at this stage
  • If someone says yes to pilot: deploy immediately and skip to Phase 2 gate. You've earned it.
Retrospective
  • Write Phase 1 review in Journal: What did you build? What worked in outreach? What flopped? What would you do differently in Week 1–6?
  • Gate check — be honest: Is the demo live and working? Did a real owner conversation happen and get logged? Both yes → Phase 2. One or both no → name the specific gap and fix it this week before advancing.
  • Celebrate what you actually built. A working AI automation and a real outreach campaign in 6 weeks at 5 hrs/week is real. Acknowledge it before moving.

Objection Handling
"We already have software that does this."
→ "Does it automatically follow up on every missed call, triage urgency with AI, and send a Google review request 3 days after every completed job?" Most shop management systems don't. That's the gap I fill. I'm not replacing your software — I'm adding the intelligence layer on top of it.
"We're too small for something like this."
→ "That's actually why this works so well for you." A 2-truck shop missing 3 emergency calls a week loses $15–20K in annual revenue at $450/call. This pays for itself in the first month if it catches 2 jobs you'd have lost.
"What if it breaks?"
→ "That's what the retainer covers." I monitor it, fix errors same day, and you get a monthly check-in. If something breaks and I don't catch it in 24 hours, your next month is free. That's the service agreement.
"We tried automation before and it didn't work."
→ "What went wrong?" (Let them answer.) Usually it's that someone set it up once and never maintained it, or it was too generic. This is configured specifically for how your shop operates, and I manage it ongoing. That's different from a one-time tool setup.
"I need to think about it / talk to my partner."
→ "That makes sense. What's the main thing you'd want to talk through?" Get the real objection out. Then: "Want me to set up a 15-minute call with both of you so you can see it live?" Give them a deadline: "I can hold your spot in the onboarding queue until [specific date]."
